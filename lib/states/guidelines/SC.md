# South Carolina — High School NIL Guidelines

**Status:** Not permitted
**Last verified:** 2026-04-29
**Primary source:** SCHSL Constitution & Bylaws, Article II (Amateur Status), as amended June 2025 — [link](https://schsl.org/archives/16430)

## Overview

South Carolina is one of the hardest "no" states in the country for high school NIL. In June 2025, the South Carolina High School League (SCHSL) amended its bylaws to explicitly prohibit student-athletes from earning compensation for their athletic participation. The change was forced by a clause in the state budget that bars public school districts from affiliating with any athletic association that "permits, allows or authorizes" NIL compensation. Until June 2025, SCHSL's bylaws had ambiguous language that some read as allowing NIL — that ambiguity is now gone.

## Who can participate

**No one.** If you compete in a SCHSL-sanctioned sport at a public or member school, you are an amateur and you may not accept any compensation, gift, or in-kind benefit tied to your name, image, or likeness as an athlete. The previous bylaw said students "may earn compensation"; the new bylaw says students "may not earn compensation." Family members (by blood or marriage) are also barred from signing on your behalf.

If you accept an NIL deal, you immediately lose your amateur status — and therefore your eligibility to compete.

## Before you sign a deal

Don't. Any NIL agreement you enter while you have remaining SCHSL eligibility puts that eligibility at risk. This includes paid social posts, brand sponsorships, autograph fees, group licensing, and collective payments. The bylaws make no carve-out for non-athletic compensation that uses your athletic identity, and the safest read is: if a brand is paying you because you are a high school athlete, it is prohibited.

If you are training for college recruitment and a college program asks about NIL, **stop and talk to your athletic director first.** SCHSL has not created a college-bound exception comparable to Texas's UIL rule.

## When you sign a deal — your obligations

Because deals are not permitted, NILPro will block deal flow for South Carolina athletes. If you believe you have a unique situation (Olympic-track non-school competition, modeling unrelated to athletics, etc.), follow this sequence:

1. **Day 0 — Pause.** Do not sign anything.
2. **Day 0–2 — Contact your school AD in writing.** Describe the proposed activity in detail.
3. **Day 0–7 — Get a written ruling.** Your AD or principal must consult SCHSL before clearing anything that touches your athletic identity.
4. **Keep records.** Save every email and ruling for the duration of your high school career.

## Banned deal categories

Effectively all categories. The bylaw bans NIL compensation outright. Even categories permitted in other states — youth coaching for pay using your athlete identity, signed-merchandise sales, paid camp appearances — are off-limits if they reference or rely on your status as a SCHSL athlete.

## Penalties for non-compliance

- **First violation:** Formal warning; return of any awards or money received; mandatory removal of any endorsement or promotional content.
- **Second violation:** One-year ineligibility from SCHSL interscholastic athletics.
- **Third violation:** Penalties at the Executive Director's discretion based on the nature, severity, and intent of the conduct.

## What NILPro does — and what we don't

NILPro provides you with the rules, deadlines, and templates you need to comply with South Carolina's HS NIL requirements. **We do not act on your behalf with your school, your AD, or businesses.** All disclosures and communications must come from you, sent from your own email or contact information. NILPro is software — not your agent or representative.
