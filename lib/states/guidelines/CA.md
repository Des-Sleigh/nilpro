# California — High School NIL Guidelines

**Status:** Permitted with restrictions
**Last verified:** 2026-04-29
**Primary source:** CIF Constitution & Bylaws — Bylaw 510 (Amateur Status / NIL) and related guidance — [link](https://www.cifstate.org/governance/constitution/index)

## Overview

California was effectively the first state to permit high school NIL, and the California Interscholastic Federation (CIF) governs the rules. CIF Bylaw 510, together with the CIF "Do's and Don'ts" guidance, allows student-athletes to monetize their NIL only as individuals — never as representatives of their school or team. The rule is built around a clean firewall between the personal brand and the school brand: you can promote products as yourself; you cannot promote products as a Cobra, Knight, or Trojan in jersey, on campus, or at a school event.

## Who can participate

Any student-athlete at a CIF member high school can participate, across all 10 CIF sections and all sanctioned sports. CIF jurisdiction covers grades 9-12. There is no minimum age for the bylaw itself, but California contract law requires a parent or legal guardian to sign any binding agreement on behalf of an athlete under 18. Middle-school athletes are not covered by Bylaw 510 and should consult their district directly.

## Before you sign a deal

CIF guidance is unusually direct: read the contract and confirm you can perform every deliverable without using a high school uniform, school name, mascot, logo, school facility, or anything that ties the activity to your school or team. If the brand wants you in uniform, on the field, or with school colors, you cannot do the deal as-is — you would need a separate license from the school, which CIF strongly discourages. Confirm the deal is not an enrollment incentive (no "stay at this school and we'll pay you") — that triggers Bylaw 510's "undue influence" prohibition. If you are under 18, plan for parental co-signature.

## When you sign a deal — your obligations

1. **Disclose to your athletic director within 7 days of signing.** Submit a written notice including the business name, deal terms, dates, and a copy of the contract. CIF does not mandate a single statewide deadline, but 7 days is the standard CIF section practice and many CIF sections require faster.
2. **Have a parent or legal guardian co-sign if you are under 18.** Required by California contract law.
3. **Avoid school marks, uniforms, and facilities in all deal-related content.** No school name, no school logos, no monikers, no insignias, no jerseys, no on-campus shoots — including social media posts that reference your team.
4. **Do not represent yourself as part of a school team in any promotional material.** Promote as an individual athlete, not "as a member of [School] [Sport]."
5. **Keep a copy of the signed contract and your disclosure email for at least four years.** CIF sections may audit.

## Banned deal categories

Bylaw 510's "undue influence" provision bans any compensation that functions as an enrollment or transfer inducement — protecting competitive balance across CIF schools. CIF guidance and most CIF sections also prohibit deals involving alcohol, tobacco/vaping, cannabis, controlled substances, gambling and sports betting, adult entertainment, and firearms; these protect minors from products they cannot legally purchase. School-affiliated boosters and collectives may not use NIL deals to recruit or retain athletes.

## Penalties for non-compliance

Violations of Bylaw 510 typically result in loss of amateur status and ineligibility — at the section's discretion, this can range from a multi-game suspension to ineligibility for the remainder of the season. The athlete may be required to return any compensation. CIF sections can impose additional penalties on the school if administrators were aware of the violation. Late or missed disclosures are themselves violations.
