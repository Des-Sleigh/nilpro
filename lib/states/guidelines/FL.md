# Florida — High School NIL Guidelines

**Status:** Permitted with restrictions
**Last verified:** 2026-04-29
**Primary source:** FHSAA Bylaw 9.9 — Amateurism & NIL (effective July 24, 2024) — [link](https://fhsaa.com/documents/2024/6/4/Bylaw_9_9_Voted_Language.pdf?id=5510)

## Overview

Florida became the 36th state to permit high school NIL when the Florida State Board of Education ratified FHSAA Bylaw 9.9 on July 24, 2024. The Florida High School Athletic Association governs all member-school athletics, and Bylaw 9.9 sets the framework: athletes can sign commercial NIL deals, but the bylaw imposes some of the most explicit minor-protection requirements in the country. The headline rule for parents: **any NIL contract with a Florida high school student-athlete under 18 must be signed by a parent or legal guardian** — co-signature is mandatory, not optional.

## Who can participate

All student-athletes at FHSAA member schools may participate, across every sanctioned sport. Bylaw 9.9 applies to grades 9-12 (and any FHSAA-sanctioned middle-school program where applicable). NIL agreements are explicitly limited to the student-athlete's high school period of athletic eligibility — they cannot extend beyond the athlete's high school graduation date. Out-of-state students enrolled at FHSAA schools are subject to the same rule.

## Before you sign a deal

Read the contract with your parent or legal guardian. Florida law requires their signature for any binding contract with a minor, and Bylaw 9.9 reinforces this. For higher-value contracts, Florida Statute 743.08 may require judicial approval through circuit court — talk to a lawyer if the deal is substantial. Confirm the contract does not extend beyond your graduation date (categorically banned), is not contingent on athletic performance, is not an enrollment or transfer incentive, and is not from a school, booster, or NIL collective. Confirm no use of school marks or facilities. Confirm the brand and product category are not in the banned list below.

## When you sign a deal — your obligations

1. **Get your parent or legal guardian to co-sign the contract — required for every athlete under 18.** This is the most distinctive feature of Florida's framework. A contract not co-signed by the parent or guardian is not a valid NIL contract under Bylaw 9.9.
2. **Submit the deal disclosure to your school within 5 business days of signing.** The student/parent must complete, obtain all applicable signatures, and submit the school's NIL disclosure form within 5 business days of signing the agreement.
3. **Use no school uniforms, equipment, logos, names, patents, products, or copyrights in deal-related content.** Written consent and licensing from the school is required for any such use, and that licensing rarely happens at the HS level.
4. **Confirm every contract terminates at or before your graduation date.** Bylaw 9.9 prohibits NIL agreements that extend past graduation.
5. **Save the signed contract, parent co-signature, school disclosure form, and any payment records for at least four years.** The FHSAA may audit.

## Banned deal categories

Bylaw 9.9 lists explicit prohibited categories: adult entertainment products and services; alcohol, tobacco, vaping, and nicotine; cannabis and controlled substances; prescription pharmaceuticals; gambling, including sports betting and the lottery; weapons and firearms; and — uniquely to Florida — NIL collectives. The collective ban is intentional: Florida lawmakers and the FHSAA wanted to prevent the college-style "donor pool pays athletes" model from arriving at the high school level, where it would distort recruiting. Each banned category exists to protect minors from products they cannot legally buy or from arrangements that would compromise amateur athletics.

## Penalties for non-compliance

A confirmed Bylaw 9.9 violation results in loss of amateur status and ineligibility in the sport in which the violation occurred, with the FHSAA setting the duration. The athlete may be required to return compensation received from the non-compliant deal. Schools that knew of and permitted a violation can face forfeitures and additional FHSAA sanctions. A late disclosure (after the 5 business-day window) or a missing parent co-signature is itself a violation, even if the underlying deal would otherwise have been compliant.
