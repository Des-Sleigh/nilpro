# Wyoming — High School NIL Guidelines

**Status:** Not permitted — banned by state statute
**Last verified:** 2026-04-29
**Primary source:** Wyoming Senate File 53 (2026), "Keeping Amateurism in High School Athletics," signed by Governor Mark Gordon on March 3, 2026 — [link](https://legiscan.com/WY/bill/SF0053/2026)

## Overview

**Wyoming is the hardest "no" in the country.** Unlike every other state where HS NIL prohibitions live in association bylaws (which can change with a board vote), Wyoming has codified amateurism in **state statute**. Senate File 53, sponsored by Sen. Gary Crum and signed by Governor Mark Gordon on March 3, 2026, requires amateur status for any student competing in a Wyoming High School Activities Association (WHSAA) sanctioned sport. The bill backs up the WHSAA's existing prohibition — and follows a 2025 WHSAA proposal to permit NIL that was defeated. NIL deals for Wyoming high school athletes are not just against league rules; they are against state law.

## Who can participate

**No one.** If you compete in a WHSAA-sanctioned sport, state statute requires you to maintain amateur status. You may not receive payment for your name, image, or likeness while representing a Wyoming high school. There is no age floor; the prohibition applies to every WHSAA athlete. Family members signing on your behalf does not unlock NIL — the statute focuses on the athlete's amateur status, and a payment to a family member tied to your athletic identity is treated as a payment to you.

## Before you sign a deal

Don't. Any NIL payment received while you are competing in a WHSAA-sanctioned sport breaks state law and ends your eligibility. The statute does **not** eliminate awards, trophies, travel, food, or participation benefits, and Wyoming created exceptions to allow athletes to collect Operation Gold funds (Olympic-pipeline awards). It does, however, prohibit commercial NIL.

If you are training for college recruitment and a college program asks about NIL, **stop and talk to your AD first.** Wyoming has no college-bound exception comparable to Texas's UIL rule.

## When you sign a deal — your obligations

Because deals are not permitted, NILPro will block deal flow for Wyoming high school athletes. If you believe you are in a true exception lane (Operation Gold funding, non-athletic modeling unrelated to your school sport, or a deal that fully postdates your final WHSAA competition), follow this sequence:

1. **Day 0 — Pause.** Do not sign anything.
2. **Day 0–2 — Contact your AD in writing** with a complete description of the proposed activity.
3. **Day 0–7 — Get a written ruling.** Your AD or principal must consult WHSAA. Because the rule is now statutory, schools should also consult district counsel.
4. **Keep records.** Save every email, ruling, and exception determination.

## Banned deal categories

Effectively all categories. The statute and WHSAA rules together ban NIL compensation outright while you are competing. That includes paid social posts, brand sponsorships, autograph fees, paid camp appearances tied to your athletic identity, group licensing, and collective payments. The narrow allowances for awards, trophies, travel, food, and Operation Gold are exceptions, not a category list — anything that looks like commercial NIL is prohibited.

## Penalties for non-compliance

Loss of WHSAA eligibility under association rules, with the additional weight of state statute. Because the prohibition is now codified, district legal counsel will be involved in any review, and remedies can include forfeiture of contests by your team. Reinstatement under WHSAA rules typically requires returning compensation, removing offending content, and serving an ineligibility period — but the statutory layer means schools have less discretion than in association-only states.
