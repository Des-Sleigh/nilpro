# Alabama — High School NIL Guidelines

**Status:** Not permitted
**Last verified:** 2026-04-29
**Primary source:** AHSAA Handbook, Section 8 — Amateur Rule — [link](https://www.ahsaa.com/Resources/Handbook)

## Overview

Alabama is one of the few remaining states where high school athletes cannot earn money from their name, image, or likeness while remaining eligible to play. The Alabama High School Athletic Association (AHSAA) governs all member-school athletics, and its Section 8 "Amateur Rule" defines an amateur as someone "who does not use his/her knowledge of athletics or athletic skill for gain." Any compensation tied to your athletic identity puts your eligibility at immediate risk. Executive Director Heath Harmon's office has signaled study of NIL is ongoing, but as of April 2026 no rule change has been adopted.

## Who can participate

No high school student-athlete in Alabama may participate in paid NIL activity tied to their athletic skill or identity. The prohibition applies to every AHSAA-sanctioned sport, all classifications, and every grade in grades 7-12 at member schools. There is no "minor sports" carve-out and no allowance for booster, business, or collective payments. If you are an Alabama high school athlete, the safe assumption is: zero paid endorsements, zero sponsored posts, zero appearance fees while you are eligible.

## Before you sign a deal

Don't sign anything. Signing an NIL contract — even one that pays a small amount, even one that is "just a free product" with social media obligations attached — will likely make you ineligible under Section 8 the moment you accept the benefit. If a brand, agent, or "collective" approaches you, the safest path is: (1) decline politely, (2) tell your athletic director in writing that the offer was made, and (3) keep a copy of the email or message you received. Family members and "third parties" cannot accept the benefit on your behalf either; the AHSAA treats indirect payments the same as direct ones.

## When you sign a deal — your obligations

Currently, no HS NIL deals are permitted in Alabama. If you want to prepare for college NIL (where you can earn legally once you sign a National Letter of Intent or enroll), here is what you can do today without risking eligibility:

1. **Build your personal brand without monetizing it.** You may post on social media, grow a following, and create content — as long as no compensation, free product, discount, or other "thing of value" is exchanged for it.
2. **Document offers you receive.** Keep a folder of brand outreach. When you reach college, your future agent or compliance officer will want this history.
3. **Confirm the rule annually with your athletic director.** AHSAA may revise Section 8; ask each August.
4. **Do not let a parent, sibling, or LLC accept money on your behalf.** Indirect compensation tied to your athletic identity is treated as a violation.

## Banned deal categories

Every category is effectively banned — Alabama's prohibition is total, not category-based. The reason behind Section 8 is the AHSAA's view that paid endorsement of any kind compromises the amateur status that is central to interscholastic competition and protects minors from commercial pressure.

## Penalties for non-compliance

A confirmed Section 8 violation results in immediate loss of amateur status and ineligibility in the sport in which the violation occurred. Reinstatement requires repayment or return of the compensation received and a formal review by the AHSAA's Central Board. Schools can face additional sanctions (forfeitures, fines) if they were aware of and permitted the violation.
